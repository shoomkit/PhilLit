## Key Papers and Main Arguments

### Foundational Recent Work (2023-2025)

1. **van Leeuwen (2025)** - "What is Wrong with Gentrification-Related Displacement?"
   - Critically examines the dominant autonomy-based framework for understanding displacement harms
   - Questions whether autonomy accounts adequately distinguish legitimate from illegitimate displacement
   - Most recent critical engagement with the philosophical gentrification literature

2. **Kogelmann (2025)** - "Autonomy, Zoning, and Gentrification"
   - Exposes tension: autonomy used both to justify exclusionary zoning AND to critique gentrification
   - Shows incumbent vs. newcomer conflict at heart of housing justice
   - Questions whether coherent autonomy-based housing policy is possible

3. **Dummer & Neuh채user (2025)** - "Housing Justice, Basic Capabilities, and Self-Respect"
   - Applies refined capability approach to housing, emphasizing positional and relational dimensions
   - Housing location affects comparative status and self-respect, not just absolute quality
   - Latest philosophical application of capability approach to housing

4. **Lopez-Cantero (2025)** - "Gentrification, Migration, and Non-Material Injustice"
   - Develops "phenomenological displacement" concept - staying but experiencing alienation
   - Expands displacement harms beyond physical removal to include symbolic/emotional dislocation
   - Shows compensation cannot fully remedy loss of social world and belonging

### Core Philosophical Framework Papers

5. **Huber & Wolkenstein (2018)** - "Gentrification and Occupancy Rights"
   - **FOUNDATIONAL** - Applies territorial rights theory to urban contexts
   - Introduces "located life plans" concept dominating recent displacement ethics
   - Argues long-term residents have occupancy rights grounded in place-based projects

6. **Lloyd (2023)** - "Gentrification: A Philosophical Analysis and Critique"
   - Develops Lockean argument: communities create value through joint labor over time
   - Extends Walzer's spheres of justice to include community goods
   - Focuses on collective harms (community dissolution) beyond individual displacement

7. **Wells (2019)** - "The Right to Housing"
   - Defends right to housing including right to live alone (not just communal shelter)
   - Freedom-based account emphasizing privacy, autonomy, intimate relationships
   - Ambitious claim: resource constraints don't justify forcing communal living

8. **Essert (2016)** - "Property and Homelessness"
   - Libertarian framework: homelessness is lack of property rights, not just goods
   - Property rights necessary to avoid subordination regarding where one may be
   - Controversial claim: property doesn't create homelessness, solves it

### Structural Injustice and Segregation

9. **Shelby (2016)** - "Dark Ghettos: Injustice, Dissent, and Reform"
   - Ghettos as symptoms of systemic injustice in basic structure
   - Structural racism, wealth inequality, housing discrimination as root causes
   - Calls for basic structure reform over targeted interventions

10. **Anderson (2010)** - "The Imperative of Integration"
    - Segregation causes persistent racial disadvantage across all well-being measures
    - Integration is moral imperative even when requiring neighborhood change
    - Creates tension with place-protection arguments that may entrench segregation

11. **Young (2011)** - "Responsibility for Justice"
    - Uses housing example (Sandy facing eviction) to develop structural injustice framework
    - Many actors constitute unjust housing markets without individual wrongdoing
    - Forward-looking collective responsibility to remedy systemic patterns

### Political Economy and Financialization

12. **Madden & Marcuse (2016)** - "In Defense of Housing"
    - Housing crisis rooted in commodification and financialization under capitalism
    - Gentrification and luxury development as "hypercommodification"
    - Advocates decommodification and radical right to housing

13. **Harvey (2008)** - "The Right to the City"
    - Marxist conception: right to city as collective power to reshape urbanization
    - Urban restructuring serves accumulation and dispossession, not human needs
    - Anti-capitalist alternative to liberal individual rights frameworks

14. **Aalbers (2016)** - "The Financialization of Housing"
    - Housing transformed from consumption good to financial asset (High-Quality Collateral)
    - State actively creates financialization through "regulated deregulation"
    - Links to inequality, instability, and populism

### Urban Theory and Planning

15. **Fainstein (2010)** - "The Just City"
    - Three pillars: equity, diversity, democracy (all necessary for urban justice)
    - Critiques communicative planning - procedure doesn't ensure substantive justice
    - Pragmatic egalitarian approach within capitalism

16. **Lees, Slater & Wyly (2008)** - "Gentrification"
    - **FOUNDATIONAL EMPIRICAL WORK** - Comprehensive gentrification analysis
    - Documents displacement, challenges benign narratives
    - Establishes critical gentrification studies tradition

### Empirical Foundation

17. **Desmond (2016)** - "Evicted"
    - **PULITZER PRIZE** - Eviction as cause, not consequence, of poverty
    - Ethnographic documentation of exploitation through serial eviction
    - Advocates universal housing vouchers and tenant protections

### Recent Innovations (2024-2025)

18. **Erck (2024)** - "Housing Limitarianism"
    - Novel application: it's wrong to have too much housing while others lack enough
    - Housing wealth ceilings complement right to housing
    - Addresses supply side by limiting multiple-property ownership

19. **Draper (2024)** - "Gentrification and Everyday Democracy"
    - Gentrification hinders everyday democratic communication
    - Two mechanisms: community disintegration in enclaves, homogenization in public spaces
    - Democratic harm distinct from displacement or welfare loss

20. **Christmas (2025)** - "Free to Build"
    - Libertarian case for loosening development restrictions
    - Rights over commonly-used urban space have liberty justification
    - Challenges incumbent-protection by emphasizing newcomers' liberty claims

21. **Halliday & Meyer (2024)** - "Justice and Housing" (Survey)
    - Comprehensive overview of philosophical housing justice literature
    - Maps connections between rights, capabilities, freedom, structural approaches
    - Essential reference for field's current state

## Major Positions on BOTH Sides

### Anti-Gentrification / Pro-Incumbent Protection

**Key Arguments**:
- **Autonomy-based**: Displacement violates located life plans and place-based autonomy (Huber & Wolkenstein, van Leeuwen)
- **Community goods**: Joint labor creates collective value deserving protection (Lloyd)
- **Non-material harms**: Phenomenological displacement, loss of belonging (Lopez-Cantero)
- **Democratic**: Gentrification undermines everyday democratic communication (Draper)
- **Structural**: Class dispossession and capitalist accumulation (Harvey, Madden & Marcuse)

**Scholars**: Huber, Wolkenstein, Lloyd, Lopez-Cantero, van Leeuwen, Draper, Slater, Harvey, Madden, Marcuse

**Strengths**:
- Captures temporal dimension: established residents develop attachments, projects, relationships over time
- Recognizes non-material values (community, belonging, democratic culture)
- Identifies collective/structural harms beyond individual welfare
- Grounds claims in autonomy, not just welfare, elevating normative status

**Challenges**:
- May protect segregation and exclusion (tension with Anderson)
- Difficulty distinguishing legitimate from illegitimate displacement
- Unclear why incumbents' claims trump newcomers' equal access claims
- Can entrench privilege under guise of place-protection

### Pro-Development / Market-Oriented

**Key Arguments**:
- **Liberty-based**: Newcomers have liberty claims to build and access (Christmas)
- **Aggregate benefits**: Gentrification brings revitalization, economic growth, reduced poverty (though displaced people disappear from statistics)
- **Supply constraints**: Housing crisis caused by restrictive zoning, NIMBYism (Kogelmann, Christmas)
- **Integration**: Breaking segregation requires neighborhood change (Anderson)
- **Property rights**: Secure property rights necessary to solve homelessness (Essert)

**Scholars**: Christmas, Essert, Anderson (on integration), Kogelmann (critical analysis)

**Strengths**:
- Addresses supply-side constraints and artificial scarcity
- Challenges incumbent veto over newcomer access
- Integration argument has strong racial justice foundation
- Highlights newcomers' claims often ignored in displacement debates

**Challenges**:
- Aggregate benefits often accrue to different people than costs
- Ignores non-material harms and community dissolution
- May facilitate gentrification under guise of housing access
- Underestimates place-attachment's moral significance

### Middle Ground / Alternative Frameworks

**Right to Housing Advocates**:
- Housing as basic right or capability regardless of market (Wells, Dummer & Neuh채user, Desmond, Madden & Marcuse)
- State obligation to ensure housing access
- Neither incumbent nor newcomer claims settle issue - all have housing rights
- Focus on provision, not just distribution of existing stock

**Structural Injustice Theorists**:
- Focus on systemic patterns, not individual rights/wrongs (Young, Shelby)
- Housing injustice reflects basic structure failures
- Collective responsibility to remedy structural patterns
- Integration and desegregation as structural imperatives (Anderson)

**Capability Approach**:
- Housing quality relative to others matters for self-respect and capabilities (Dummer & Neuh채user)
- Positional goods require distributional principles beyond sufficiency
- Links housing to fundamental conditions for equal citizenship

**Limitarianism**:
- Upper bounds on housing wealth complement lower bounds (Erck)
- Excessive ownership wrongful when others lack enough
- Addresses concentration and hoarding, not just provision

## Gaps and Connections to Other Domains

### Notable Gaps

1. **Temporal Structure Under-Theorized**: Most work focuses on synchronic distribution or current rights, not diachronic entitlement evolution. How do housing claims strengthen/weaken over time? When does residence create occupancy rights?

2. **Climate Migration**: Minimal philosophical work connecting climate displacement to housing justice. How do climate refugee claims interact with incumbent housing claims in climate-safe cities?

3. **Exclusionary Zoning Ethics**: Kogelmann begins this, but limited sustained philosophical analysis of zoning's moral status. When is exclusion wrongful vs. legitimate community self-determination?

4. **Comparative Justice**: Most work examines single-city justice. What about justice across cities/regions? Can place-protection in one city create injustice elsewhere?

5. **Integration vs. Place-Protection Tension**: Anderson and Huber/Wolkenstein point opposite directions. How to balance desegregation imperatives with incumbent protection?

### Connections to Other Domains

**Domain 1 (Temporal Claims & Distributive Justice)**:
- **Core connection**: Housing incumbency provides paradigm case of temporal priority claims
- Occupancy rights arguments ground entitlements in historical presence/attachment
- Tensions between temporal priority and equal access
- **Key question**: Do temporal claims from prior residence trump equal access rights?

**Domain 2 (Climate Justice & Migration)**:
- **Connection**: Climate migration will intensify housing competition in climate-safe cities
- Rising housing costs may exclude climate refugees from safe regions
- Incumbent vs. newcomer tension amplified when newcomers are climate refugees
- **Key question**: Do climate refugees' displacement claims override incumbents' place-attachment claims?

**Potential Domain 4 (Time-Slice vs. Historical Principles)**:
- Housing justice literatures implicitly takes historical view - current holdings reflect past injustice (segregation, discrimination, redlining)
- Connects to Nozick's entitlement theory and rectification of past injustice
- **Key question**: Does housing's history of injustice require more than time-slice redistribution?

**Cross-Cutting Themes**:
- **Commodification**: When is market allocation inappropriate for housing as basic need?
- **Structural injustice**: How do institutional patterns create systemic housing injustice?
- **Non-material values**: Community, belonging, democratic culture vs. material welfare
- **Temporal priority**: Does established residence create stronger claims than equal access?

## Synthesis Recommendations

1. **Frame Around Incumbent-Newcomer Tension**: This is the CENTRAL conflict connecting to temporal claims project. Housing provides concrete case study where temporal priority (incumbents) conflicts with equal access (newcomers).

2. **Distinguish Levels of Analysis**:
   - Individual rights (Wells, Essert, Huber & Wolkenstein)
   - Community goods (Lloyd, Draper)
   - Structural patterns (Young, Shelby, Anderson)

3. **Map Normative Framework Variations**:
   - Rights-based (Wells, Harvey)
   - Capability approach (Dummer & Neuh채user)
   - Freedom/autonomy (Essert, Wells, Huber & Wolkenstein)
   - Structural injustice (Young, Shelby)
   - Political economy (Harvey, Madden & Marcuse, Aalbers)
   - Show how different frameworks generate different conclusions

4. **Integration-Protection Tradeoff is CRUCIAL**:
   - Anderson: integration requires neighborhood change, may override place-attachment
   - Huber & Wolkenstein: occupancy rights protect incumbents from displacement
   - **This tension is UNRESOLVED and central to housing justice**

5. **Empirical Grounding Essential**:
   - Desmond's eviction research
   - Lees/Slater/Wyly's gentrification documentation
   - Shelby's segregation analysis
   - Don't let philosophy float free from empirical reality

6. **Recent Work (2020-2025) Shows Field Maturation**:
   - Multiple papers in top philosophy journals
   - Sophisticated normative frameworks beyond simple welfare or rights
   - Growing attention to non-material harms, structural patterns, capabilities
   - Field moving from niche to mainstream philosophical concern

7. **Political Economy Critical But Contested**:
   - Left scholars (Harvey, Madden & Marcuse, Aalbers) see housing crisis as structural feature of capitalism
   - Others work within capitalist frameworks seeking reforms
   - **Fundamental disagreement**: Can housing justice be achieved through market reforms, or does it require decommodification?

## Key Takeaways for Research Project

1. **Housing provides IDEAL case study for temporal claims research**: Incumbency grounds claims in historical presence, attachment formation, community creation - all temporal processes

2. **The autonomy-based framework dominates but faces challenges**: Van Leeuwen's critique shows limits; Kogelmann reveals internal tensions; needs refinement

3. **Material vs. non-material harms**: Housing justice includes welfare but also belonging, community, democratic culture, self-respect - richer normative landscape

4. **Structural vs. individual analysis**: Young and Shelby show limits of individual-responsibility models; housing injustice is systemic

5. **Unresolved tension: protection vs. access**: Place-protection arguments may entrench privilege/segregation; access arguments may facilitate displacement. No clear resolution.

6. **Climate connection under-explored**: Housing justice literature doesn't adequately address climate migration implications. This is a GAP the research project could address.

7. **Philosophical work grounded in empirical reality**: Desmond, Lees/Slater/Wyly, Anderson provide essential empirical foundations. Philosophy can't proceed in empirical vacuum.

8. **Multiple normative frameworks available**: Rights, capabilities, freedom, structural injustice, political economy each illuminate different dimensions. Synthesis requires acknowledging pluralism.
