## Section 1: Introduction and Measurement Debates (H6)

**Word Target**: 400-500 words

**Primary Hypothesis**: H6 - Housing burden operationalization is normatively charged and affects findings

**Purpose**:
- Establish that measurement choices fundamentally shape burden identification
- Set up empirical sections by clarifying measurement heterogeneity across studies
- Frame the review as addressing both substantive questions (H1-H5) AND methodological questions (H6)

**Key Papers to Cite** (Domain 5 + relevant Domain 1):
- **Stone2006/Stone2006b** - Ratio vs. residual income approach (62% vs. 48% burdened)
- **HerbertHermann2013** - Assessing the 30% standard, historical contingency
- **LiXu2022** - Subjective vs. objective burden, imperfect overlap
- **Haffner2021/Haffner2021b** - Measurement as normatively laden choice
- **FrickKrell2010** - SOEP vs. EU-SILC measurement differences
- **Eurostat2024** - EU-SILC methodology (40% overburden threshold, equivalisation)
- **OECD2020** - Cross-national measurement standardization
- **Whitehead2021** - Measurement choices affect policy evaluation

**Structure**:
1. **Opening** (100 words): Frame housing cost burden as both empirical and methodological question. German context: 54% renters, 12% overburdened (>40% income), rising inequality.

2. **Measurement Debates** (250 words):
   - **Ratio vs. residual income**: Stone shows 62% burdened by residual approach vs. 48% by 30% ratio. Choice affects who counts as burdened.
   - **Subjective vs. objective**: Li & Xu show imperfect overlap - some with high objective burden don't feel burdened, vice versa
   - **Threshold choices**: 30% vs. 40%, equivalisation methods (OECD scale), gross vs. net income
   - **Data sources**: SOEP vs. EU-SILC produce different distributions (Frick & Krell)
   - **Normative assumptions**: Haffner & Hulse - no measurement is value-neutral, reflects policy priorities

3. **Implications for This Review** (100 words):
   - Following sections report findings as stated in original studies
   - Note measurement approach when relevant to interpretation
   - Most German/European studies use ratio approach with 30% or 40% thresholds
   - Measurement heterogeneity itself is research finding (H6 supported)

**Quantified Findings to Highlight**:
- Stone: 62.1% burdened (residual) vs. 47.9% (30% ratio) - 14-point difference
- EU-SILC: 40% equivalised income threshold (standard overburden definition)
- Germany: 12% overburdened (Destatis 2024) vs. EU average 8.2%

## Section 2: Housing Burden Distribution - The Income Gradient (H1, H2)

**Word Target**: 1000-1200 words

**Primary Hypotheses**:
- H1 - Flat burdens for median incomes despite rising urban costs?
- H2 - High burdens for low-income city dwellers

**Purpose**:
- Document income gradient in housing burden (H2 strongly supported)
- Test whether median-income households maintain stable burdens in high-cost cities (H1 - mixed evidence)
- Show spatial dimension: urban vs. rural, cross-city variation
- Quantify burden levels by income quintile

**Key Papers to Cite** (Domains 1, 4):

**Core Burden Distribution Studies**:
- **Dustmann2022** - FOUNDATIONAL: Housing amplifies inequality 3x (22pp→62pp), bottom quintile burden rising, top falling
- **Destatis2024** - 12% Germany overburdened vs. 8.2% EU average
- **OECD2022** - 10+ point burden difference between bottom and third quintile across 26 countries
- **IMF2023** - Almost half of poorest households overburdened regardless of tenure
- **Dewilde2024** - 27 European countries, market renters' position deteriorated 2010-2018
- **DIW2021** - Two-thirds of elderly renter households have 30%+ burden, 38% have 40%+
- **EuropeanParliament2024** - 38.2% burden for low-income vs. 16.2% for higher-income (EU 2023)

**Urban Housing Markets**:
- **EmpiricaRegio2024** - 31% burden for low-income, 24% for median income, 70% rent increase 2011-2024 in A-cities
- **Stawarz2021/Stawarz2021b** - Rent-to-income ratios increase with city size, 26% cumulative rent increase 2011-2017, 4%/year
- **Berger2023** - 15% Berlin residents experienced displacement, 40k new residents/year but only 15k new dwellings
- **DZHYP2024** - Munich 0.2% vacancy, need 320k new dwellings/year nationally but falling below target
- **GlobalProperty2025** - Berlin 160% cumulative price increase since 2000, 2% urban vacancy vs. 7.6% rural
- **Cushman2025** - Rent growth slowing (3.5% in 7 major cities 2024 vs. 6% in 2023) but still rising
- **Bundesbank2023** - Owner-occupied prices fell 5.8-9.9% YoY in Q3 2023

**Tenure and Institutional Context**:
- **Kaas2021** - 22% bottom quintile homeowners vs. 45% overall, policy factors suppress homeownership
- **Voigtlaender2009** - Low homeownership due to transfer taxes, no mortgage deductibility, extensive rental sector
- **Stephens2015/Stephens2020** - Germany's "regulated rental regime", 16%+ median household burden

**Structure**:

1. **The Income Gradient: Core Finding** (300-350 words):
   - **Dustmann et al. (2022)** as anchor: Housing amplifies inequality from 22pp to 62pp (1993-2013)
   - Bottom quintile: burden rising disproportionately, 70% renters
   - Top quintile: burden falling, only 1/3 renters
   - **European comparative context**: IMF (almost 50% of poorest overburdened), Dewilde (market renters deteriorated 2010-2018), European Parliament (38.2% vs. 16.2% burden)
   - **Germany-specific levels**: 12% overburdened (Destatis), higher than EU 8.2%
   - **Vulnerable groups**: DIW (elderly renters: 2/3 have 30%+ burden, 38% have 40%+)
   - **H2 STRONGLY SUPPORTED**: Low-income urban dwellers face severe burden across all measures

2. **Urban Housing Markets: Rising Costs and Supply Constraints** (300-350 words):
   - **Cost increases**: EmpiricaRegio (70% rent increase 2011-2024 A-cities), Stawarz (26% increase 2011-2017, 4%/year), GlobalProperty (Berlin 160% since 2000)
   - **Supply constraints**: DZHYP (need 320k/year, falling short; Munich 0.2% vacancy), Berger (Berlin: 40k new residents/15k new dwellings)
   - **Spatial divide**: GlobalProperty (2% urban vacancy vs. 7.6% rural eastern Germany)
   - **Recent moderation**: Cushman (rent growth slowing 3.5% vs. 6%), Bundesbank (owner prices down 5.8-9.9%)
   - **Displacement**: Berger (15% Berlin residents displaced)

3. **Testing H1: Flat Burdens for Median Incomes?** (250-300 words):
   - **Mixed evidence**:
     - **EmpiricaRegio**: Median-income households 24% burden in cities (below 30% threshold but rising)
     - **Stawarz**: Burden increases with city size, suggesting median incomes affected
     - **Stephens**: German/Swedish median households pay 16%+ (high by international standards)
   - **Adjustment mechanisms**:
     - **Migration**: Stawarz shows rising costs → declining inflows, increasing outflows (spatial adjustment)
     - **Tenure**: Kaas, Voigtländer show Germany's unique context (low homeownership, extensive rental sector) may enable burden shifting
     - **Displacement**: Berger shows 15% displaced → median incomes may maintain stable burdens through spatial selection
   - **H1 ASSESSMENT**: Partial support - median burdens appear more stable than low-income burdens, but spatial adjustment (migration, displacement) may explain this, not true stability in place
   - **Geographic heterogeneity**: Burden concentrated in high-cost cities (Berlin, Munich) vs. rural areas

4. **Summary** (100 words):
   - Income gradient is robust and quantitatively large (H2 strongly supported)
   - Urban housing costs rising rapidly (70% in 13 years) with severe supply constraints
   - Median-income households may maintain stable burdens through spatial adjustment rather than true stability (H1 mixed)
   - Next section examines income-side determinants (wages, dualization) to explain burden patterns

**Quantified Findings to Emphasize**:
- Dustmann: 22pp → 62pp inequality amplification through housing
- 12% Germany overburdened vs. 8.2% EU average
- 38.2% burden for low-income vs. 16.2% higher-income (EU)
- 70% rent increase 2011-2024 in A-cities
- 26% cumulative rent increase 2011-2017
- 40,000 new residents/year Berlin but only 15,000 new dwellings
- 15% Berlin residents displaced
- 2% urban vacancy vs. 7.6% rural

## Section 3: Labor Market Determinants - Wages, Dualization, and the Public Sector (H3, H4, H5)

**Word Target**: 1200-1400 words

**Primary Hypotheses**:
- H3 - Public sector wage repression affects housing burden
- H4 - 80%+ of burden change explained by wage income changes
- H5 - Housing burden determined by dualized housing and labor markets

**Purpose**:
- Document wage stagnation and decline post-2000, especially Hartz IV effects
- Examine public vs. private sector wage dynamics
- Test dualization theory as explanation for burden patterns
- Assess whether wage changes can explain 80%+ of burden variation (H4)

**Key Papers to Cite** (Domains 2, 3, 6):

**Wage Trends and Stagnation (H3, H4)**:
- **IMF2015** - Hartz IV reduced real wages ~4%, wage decline strongest at 25th/50th percentiles
- **Dustmann2014** - Wage gains didn't keep pace with productivity post-1999, labor compensation fell 61%→55% GDP
- **Dustmann2022** - Income share of housing rose disproportionally for bottom quintile (connects wages to burden)
- **Biewen2018** - De-unionization accounts for significant wage dispersion increase
- **Bosch2017** - 2% unit labor cost reduction Germany vs. 22% increase OECD (2000-2007)

**Public Sector Wages (H3)**:
- **Bundesbank2024** - Public sector 24% nominal increase 2009-2018 vs. 25% economy-wide; recent catching up 2022-2024
- **Schmidt2024** - Erosion in private sector bargaining, no erosion in public sector; dual employment (civil servants vs. employees)
- **Keller2020** - Civil servants no collective bargaining/strike rights, pay determined by government
- **IFO2020** - Public sector not attractive enough for recruiting, rigid pay scales, skilled worker scarcity
- **Boeckler2024** - 3.6 million public sector employees, TVöD covers 2.5+ million

**Collective Bargaining (H3, H4)**:
- **Addison2014** - Sectoral bargaining coverage declined 14pp west, 18pp east (1996-2009); 3-4% wage effect
- **Eichhorst2015** - Labor market duality promoted non-standard employment and low-pay expansion
- **Hassel2014** - Wage restraint in low-end services "exceptional in western Europe"

**Dualization Theory (H5)**:
- **PalierThelen2010** - FOUNDATIONAL: Dualism "explicitly underwritten by state policy" in Germany/France
- **Emmenegger2012** - THE BOOK: Continental regimes perpetuate/reinforce insider-outsider divide across three dimensions
- **Lennartz2019** - CRITICAL: Links labor market dualization to housing market dualization directly
- **Schwander2019** - Labor market dualization focuses on securely employed insiders vs. precarious outsiders
- **Biegert2017** - Continental Europe shows strongest insider-outsider divides in labor, welfare, political integration
- **Rueda2005** - Insider-outsider politics structures political conflict
- **Hauesermann2012** - Continental Europe (Germany) shows strongest employment-based dualization
- **Emmenegger2009** - Strong unions/left governments protect insiders, less responsive to outsiders
- **Marx2012** - Dualization operates through institutional complementarities across industrial relations, labor market, welfare state
- **Davidsson2015** - Unions prioritize organizational survival over broad membership

**Housing Policy (Limited Effectiveness Suggests Structural Factors Dominant)**:
- **Kholodilin2016/Kholodilin2016b** - Mietpreisbremse: 5% initial effect, vanished after 1 year
- **Thomschke2019/Thomschke2017** - Rent control effects vanish over time, implementation deficit
- **Baye2024** - Rent control promotes gentrification, amplifies supply shortage
- **Jagemann2022/Jagemann2022b** - Wohngeld effective for recipients (€190→€370 doubled) but limited reach (550k-1.1m households)
- **Habitat2021** - Social housing declined 4 million (1980s) → 1 million today
- **Brezina2011** - Social housing fell 2.6m (2002) → 1.4m (2014), 41k/year loss
- **Holm2020** - Berlin social housing 360k (1990) → 100k (2020) due to privatization

**Structure**:

1. **Wage Stagnation and Decline (2000-2015)** (300-350 words):
   - **The Hartz IV effect**: IMF (4% real wage reduction), Dustmann 2014 (wage gains lagged productivity, labor compensation 61%→55% GDP)
   - **Distributional effects**: IMF (decline strongest at 25th/50th percentiles), Biewen (de-unionization explains significant wage dispersion)
   - **International comparison**: Bosch (Germany -2% unit labor costs vs. OECD +22%, 2000-2007)
   - **Sectoral variation**: Bosch (manufacturing/finance grew faster, construction/public sector/low-end services slower)
   - **Collective bargaining decline**: Addison (14-18pp decline in sectoral coverage 1996-2009, 3-4% wage effect), Hassel ("exceptional wage restraint in low-end services")
   - **Connection to burden**: Dustmann 2022 (income share of housing rose disproportionally for bottom quintile)
   - **H4 INDIRECT SUPPORT**: Wage declines 2000-2015 coincide with burden increases, but no study directly quantifies "80%+" proportion

2. **Public Sector Wages: Mixed Evidence (H3)** (300-350 words):
   - **Wage trends**: Bundesbank (public sector 24% vs. economy 25% growth 2009-2018, recent catching up 2022-2024)
   - **Institutional structure**: Schmidt (no erosion in public sector bargaining unlike private sector), Keller (dual system: civil servants without bargaining rights vs. public employees with strong unions)
   - **Recruitment challenges**: IFO (rigid pay scales, not attractive enough vs. private sector), Boeckler (3.6m public sector, 2.5m+ covered by TVöD)
   - **Comparison with private sector**: Schmidt (private sector losing bargaining coverage, public sector maintaining), Eichhorst/Hassel (duality in private sector with low-wage expansion)
   - **H3 ASSESSMENT**:
     - **NO evidence of systematic public sector wage repression** in aggregate (Bundesbank shows comparable growth 2009-2018, recent catching up)
     - **BUT structural differences**: Civil servants face rigid pay determination (Keller), recruitment challenges suggest public wages lag market (IFO)
     - **Dualization within public sector**: Civil servants (no bargaining) vs. employees (strong bargaining)
     - **H3 MIXED/NOT SUPPORTED**: Public sector wages kept pace on average; no clear evidence public sector workers face disproportionate housing burden vs. private sector workers of similar income

3. **Dualization Theory: Linking Labor and Housing Markets (H5)** (400-500 words):
   - **Core theoretical framework**:
     - **Palier & Thelen (2010)**: Dualism "explicitly underwritten by state policy" in Germany/France since 1990s
     - **Emmenegger et al. (2012)**: Continental regimes perpetuate insider-outsider divide across industrial relations, labor market policy, welfare state
     - **Three dimensions**: Employment protection (permanent vs. temporary), wage determination (covered vs. uncovered by collective bargaining), welfare access (contribution-based vs. residual)
   - **Labor market dualization mechanisms**:
     - **Schwander (2019)**: Securely employed insiders vs. precarious outsiders with distinct interests
     - **Rueda (2005)**: Insider-outsider politics structures political conflict, social democratic parties face trade-offs
     - **Emmenegger (2009)**: Strong unions/left governments protect insiders, less responsive to outsiders
     - **Davidsson (2015)**: Unions prioritize organizational survival over broad membership
     - **Biegert (2017)**: Continental Europe shows strongest insider-outsider divides; welfare state reinforces labor market dualism (net wage gaps 5-8pp above gross)
     - **Häusermann & Schwander (2012)**: Continental Europe (Germany) shows strongest employment-based dualization
     - **Marx (2012)**: Institutional complementarities across industrial relations, labor market, welfare state
   - **Connecting labor to housing dualization**:
     - **Lennartz & Helbrecht (2020)**: CRITICAL paper directly linking labor market insider-outsider status to housing tenure and housing burden
     - **Employment precarity affects housing outcomes**: Outsiders face higher rent burdens, less access to homeownership, greater displacement risk
     - **Institutional complementarities**: Dualized labor markets + declining social housing (Brezina: 4m→1m, Holm: Berlin 360k→100k) + ineffective rent control (Kholodilin: effects vanish) = dualized housing market
   - **H5 STRONG THEORETICAL SUPPORT**:
     - Comprehensive dualization framework exists (Palier/Thelen, Emmenegger et al.)
     - Explicit labor-housing connection (Lennartz & Helbrecht)
     - Institutional complementarities reinforce dualization (Marx)
     - **BUT empirical gap**: No German study directly quantifies how labor market status (insider/outsider) predicts housing burden levels while controlling for income
     - **Indirect evidence**: Dewilde (market renters' position deteriorated), Dustmann (bottom quintile burden rising), wage stagnation literature (outsiders face wage penalties)

4. **Housing Policy Effectiveness: Limits of Housing-Side Interventions** (200-250 words):
   - **Rent control (Mietpreisbremse)**: Kholodilin (5% initial effect disappeared after 1 year), Thomschke (effects vanish over time, implementation deficit), Baye (promotes gentrification, amplifies supply shortage)
   - **Social housing decline**: Habitat (4m→1m), Brezina (41k/year loss), Holm (Berlin 360k→100k due to privatization)
   - **Housing allowance (Wohngeld)**: Jagemann (effective for recipients, €190→€370 doubled, but limited reach 550k-1.1m households)
   - **Interpretation**: Limited effectiveness of housing-side policies suggests **structural factors (wages, labor market dualization) more determinative** of burden distribution (supports H4, H5 indirectly)
   - **Policy implications**: Addressing housing burden may require labor market interventions (wage policies, reducing dualization) not just housing policies

5. **Summary: Labor Market Primacy?** (100-150 words):
   - Wage stagnation 2000-2015 coincides with burden increases (H4 plausible but not directly quantified)
   - Public sector wages kept pace on average (H3 not supported in aggregate, though civil servants face rigid determination)
   - Dualization theory provides comprehensive framework linking labor to housing markets (H5 strong theoretical support, empirical gap on direct quantification)
   - Housing policy limited effectiveness suggests structural labor market factors dominant
   - **Key research gap**: No study directly quantifies "80%+ wage explanation" (H4) or tests labor market status → housing burden controlling for income (H5)

**Quantified Findings to Emphasize**:
- IMF: Hartz IV reduced wages ~4%
- Dustmann 2014: Labor compensation 61%→55% GDP (2001-2007)
- Bosch: Germany -2% unit labor costs vs. OECD +22% (2000-2007)
- Addison: Collective bargaining coverage declined 14-18pp (1996-2009), 3-4% wage effect
- Bundesbank: Public sector 24% vs. economy 25% nominal growth (2009-2018)
- Kholodilin: Mietpreisbremse 5% effect vanished after 1 year
- Social housing: 4 million (1980s) → 1 million today
- Berlin social housing: 360,000 (1990) → 100,000 (2020)
- Wohngeld: 550k-1.1m households, €190→€370 doubled (2023)

## Section 4: Research Gaps and Synthesis

**Word Target**: 400-600 words

**Purpose**:
- Synthesize evidence on all 6 hypotheses
- Identify specific empirical gaps
- Suggest directions for future research
- Provide clear assessment of what is known vs. unknown

**Structure**:

1. **Hypothesis Assessment Summary** (200-250 words):

   **H1 (Flat median-income burdens in cities)**: MIXED EVIDENCE
   - Some evidence median burdens more stable than low-income (EmpiricaRegio: 24% vs. 31%)
   - BUT spatial adjustment mechanisms (migration, displacement) may explain, not true stability
   - Gap: No longitudinal study tracking median-income households within cities over time

   **H2 (High burdens for low-income urban dwellers)**: STRONGLY SUPPORTED
   - Robust across all studies (Dustmann, IMF, Dewilde, European Parliament)
   - Quantified: 38.2% burden for low-income vs. 16.2% higher-income (EU)
   - Income gradient amplifies inequality 3x through housing (Dustmann)

   **H3 (Public sector wage repression)**: NOT SUPPORTED IN AGGREGATE
   - Public sector wages kept pace 2009-2018 (Bundesbank)
   - Institutional differences (civil servants vs. employees) but no aggregate repression
   - Gap: No study directly compares housing burden of public vs. private sector workers at same income levels

   **H4 (80%+ wage explanation)**: PLAUSIBLE BUT NOT QUANTIFIED
   - Wage stagnation 2000-2015 coincides with burden increases
   - Housing amplifies income inequality (Dustmann)
   - Gap: **No decomposition study quantifying wage vs. rent contributions to burden change**

   **H5 (Dualization explains burden)**: STRONG THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK, EMPIRICAL GAP
   - Comprehensive theory (Palier/Thelen, Emmenegger et al.)
   - Direct labor-housing connection (Lennartz & Helbrecht)
   - Gap: **No German study tests labor market status → housing burden controlling for income**

   **H6 (Measurement normatively charged)**: STRONGLY SUPPORTED
   - Ratio vs. residual: 14-point difference (Stone)
   - Subjective vs. objective: imperfect overlap (Li & Xu)
   - Measurement heterogeneity itself is key finding

2. **Critical Research Gaps** (150-200 words):

   **Decomposition Analysis (H4)**:
   - Need variance decomposition: What % of housing burden variation explained by wage changes vs. rent changes vs. household composition changes?
   - Dustmann 2022 shows housing amplifies inequality but doesn't quantify relative contributions
   - Requires longitudinal data tracking same households over time

   **Labor Market Status and Housing Burden (H5)**:
   - Need study testing: Does labor market insider/outsider status predict housing burden after controlling for income level?
   - Lennartz & Helbrecht establish conceptual link but don't provide German quantification
   - Requires combining SOEP employment data with housing cost data

   **Public vs. Private Sector Burden (H3)**:
   - Need study comparing housing burdens of public sector workers vs. private sector workers at same income levels
   - Schmidt/Keller establish institutional differences but don't test burden outcomes
   - Requires sector-specific analysis in SOEP or EU-SILC

   **Median-Income Stability in Cities (H1)**:
   - Need longitudinal study tracking median-income households within cities (not cross-sectional snapshots)
   - Current evidence confounded by spatial selection (migration, displacement)
   - Requires residential mobility analysis

3. **Synthesis: What Explains Housing Burden?** (100-150 words):

   **Strong Evidence**:
   - Income level is primary determinant (H2 robust)
   - Measurement choices matter fundamentally (H6 robust)
   - Urban housing costs rising rapidly (70% in 13 years)
   - Dualization theory provides comprehensive framework (H5 theory strong)

   **Plausible but Unquantified**:
   - Wage stagnation contributes substantially to burden increases (H4 plausible)
   - Labor market status affects housing outcomes (H5 conceptual support)

   **Not Supported**:
   - Public sector wage repression as primary driver (H3 not supported)
   - Median-income burden stability in high-cost cities (H1 mixed, confounded)

   **The Answer**: Housing burden is determined primarily by income level, with burden concentrated among low-income urban renters. Wage stagnation 2000-2015 likely contributed substantially, and dualization theory provides plausible framework, but precise quantification lacking. Housing-side policies (rent control, social housing) show limited effectiveness, suggesting structural labor market factors may be more determinative.
